Theorem3.3.1

Let \(a_1,a_2\) and \(n\) be integers with \(a_1\) and \(a_2\) not both zero. The linear Diophantine equation \(a_1x_1+a_2x_2=n\) has a solution if and only if \(\gcd(a_1,a_2)\) divides \(n\text{.}\)

If \(\gcd(a_1,a_2)\) divides \(n\) and \((x,y)\) is a solution of the equation \(a_1x+a_2y=\gcd(a_1,a_2)\) provided by the Euclidean algorithm, then

\begin{equation*} \left(\frac{xn}{\gcd(a_1,a_2)},\frac{yn}{\gcd(a_1,a_2)}\right) \end{equation*}

is a solution of the equation \(a_1x_1+a_2x_2=n\text{.}\)

If \((u_1,u_2)\) is a solution of the equation \(a_1x_1+a_2x_2=n\text{,}\) then

\begin{equation*} \left(u_1-\frac{a_2}{\gcd(a_1,a_2)}t, u_2+\frac{a_1}{\gcd(a_1,a_2)}t\right), t\in\mathbb{Z} \end{equation*}

are solutions of the equation \(a_1x_1+a_2x_2=n\text{.}\)
