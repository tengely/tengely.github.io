This is the induction hypothesis. Now we prove that the statement holds for \(n\text{,}\) as well. Consider the sum \(\sum_{k=m}^{n} \binom{k}{m}\text{:}\)

\begin{align*} \amp \sum_{k=m}^{n} \binom{k}{m} = \underbrace{\binom{m}{m} + \binom{m+1}{m} + \dots + \binom{n-1}{m}}_{= \binom{n}{m+1}, \text{ by the induction hypothesis } } + \binom{n}{m}\\ \amp = \binom{n}{m+1} + \binom{n}{m} = \binom{n+1}{m+1}\text{.} \end{align*} in-context
