ExerciseÂ 3.2.3 Suppose the opposite of the statement, that is, there exists an integer \(n\) for which \(n^2-2\) is divisible by 4. Then for some \(k\in\mathbb{Z}\) we have \(n^2-2=4k\text{.}\) Hence \(n^2=2(2k+1)\text{.}\) It follows that \(n\) is even, so \(n=2n_1\) for some integer \(n_1\text{.}\) We obtain that

\begin{equation*} 4n_1^2=2(2k+1)\text{.} \end{equation*}

After dividing the equation by 2 we get

\begin{equation*} 2(n_1^2-k)=1\text{,} \end{equation*}

a contradiction since 2 does not divide 1.
