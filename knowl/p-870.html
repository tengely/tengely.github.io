ExerciseÂ 3.1.10 Let \(S(n)\) be the statement that

\begin{equation*} \sum_{i=1}^n\frac{1}{i(i+1)}=\frac{n}{n+1}\text{.} \end{equation*}

If \(n=1\text{,}\) then the left-hand side is

\begin{equation*} \sum_{i=1}^1\frac{1}{i(i+1)}=\frac{1}{2}\text{,} \end{equation*}

and the right-hand side is \(\frac{1}{2}\text{,}\) hence \(S(1)\) is true. Assume that \(S(k)\) is true for some \(1\leq k\in\mathbb{N}\text{.}\) The statement \(S(k+1)\) says that

\begin{equation*} \sum_{i=1}^{k+1}\frac{1}{i(i+1)}=\frac{k+1}{k+2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

On the other hand, by the induction hypothesis

\begin{align*} \amp \sum_{i=1}^{k+1}\frac{1}{i(i+1)}=\left(\sum_{i=1}^{k}\frac{1}{i(i+1)}\right)+\frac{1}{(k+1)(k+2)}=\\ \amp =\frac{k}{k+1}+\frac{1}{(k+1)(k+2)}=\frac{k(k+2)+1}{(k+1)(k+2)}=\\ \amp =\frac{(k+1)^2}{(k+1)(k+2)}=\frac{k+1}{k+2}\text{.} \end{align*}

Therefore \(S(k+1)\) is true and the identity

\begin{equation*} \sum_{i=1}^n\frac{1}{i(i+1)}=\frac{n}{n+1} \end{equation*}

is valid for all positive integers \(n\text{.}\)
