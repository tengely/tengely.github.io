Now, we prove that the sum of the numbers in the \(n\)th row of Pascal's triangle is \(2^n\) by induction on \(n\text{.}\) The statement holds for \(n=0\) and \(n=1\) (in fact, we just calculated that it holds for \(n\leq 7\)). Assume now that the statement holds for \(n\text{,}\) as well. That is, the sum of the numbers in the \(n\)th row is \(2^{n}\text{.}\) Consider the sum of the \((n+1)\)st row, and let us use the generating rule of Pascal's triangle:

\begin{align*} \amp \binom{n+1}{0} + \binom{n+1}{1} + \binom{n+1}{2} + \dots + \binom{n+1}{n-1} + \binom{n+1}{n} + \binom{n+1}{n+1}\\ \amp = \binom{n}{0} + \left( \binom{n}{0} + \binom{n}{1} \right) + \left( \binom{n}{1} + \binom{n}{2} \right) + \left( \binom{n}{2} + \binom{n}{3} \right) + \dots\\ \amp + \left( \binom{n}{n-2} + \binom{n}{n-1} \right) + \left( \binom{n}{n-1} + \binom{n}{n} \right) + \binom{n}{n}\\ \amp = 2 \cdot \left[ \binom{n}{0} + \binom{n}{1} + \binom{n}{2} + \dots + \binom{n}{n-2} + \binom{n}{n-1} + \binom{n}{n} \right]\\ \amp = 2 \cdot 2^{n} = 2^{n+1}\text{.} \end{align*} in-context
